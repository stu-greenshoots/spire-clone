Spire Ascent is an ambitious browser-based love letter to MegaCrit's legendary Slay the Spire. Built entirely in React, this deck-building roguelike aims to capture the essence of card combat with AI-generated art and a deep content library. We went hands-on with the latest build to see how it stacks up.

First Impressions 7/10

Loading up Spire Ascent greets you with a slick, dark-themed main menu that punches well above its weight for an indie browser game. The title renders in a warm golden glow with feature badges highlighting the game's content: 60+ cards, 25+ enemies, 40+ relics, 3 acts, and upgradeable cards. The "Inspired by Slay the Spire" attribution at the bottom is a nice touch of transparency.

One minor gripe: the extremely dark colour palette (background is RGB 10, 10, 15) means the game essentially appears as a black rectangle on most monitors until your eyes adjust. Cranking up brightness helps, but it's a barrier to that crucial first impression. A "DATA EDITOR" button is also visible on the main menu - a developer tool that probably shouldn't be this prominent in the player-facing build.

Editor's Note
The dark theme is so aggressive that JPEG screenshots of the game initially appeared completely black in our capture tool. You genuinely need to squint at this one.

Map & Navigation 6/10

The map screen uses an SVG-based node graph that clearly displays the branching paths ahead. Node types are well-differentiated with distinct icons and colour coding: red for enemies, gold stars for elites, green campfires for rest sites, teal bags for shops, and purple chalices for events. A helpful legend at the bottom ensures nothing is ambiguous.

However, map navigation has some quirks. The game only highlights one available node at a time despite multiple valid paths being visible, making it feel overly linear. The vertical scrolling required to see the full 15-floor act can be disorienting - there's no mini-map or position indicator beyond the "Floor X" text in the header. Path connections between nodes are also drawn as simple lines with no visual weight, making it hard to trace which paths branch where.

Dev insight: The map is rendered entirely in SVG with click handlers on <g> elements. Only "available" nodes receive the pointer cursor, which is a smart accessibility pattern. Floor numbers are labelled on the left axis.

Combat 7/10

This is where Spire Ascent shines brightest. The combat system is a faithful implementation of the Slay the Spire formula: draw 5 cards, spend energy, play attacks and skills, end turn, repeat. Cards display their energy cost, type (Attack/Skill), description, and a calculated damage value at the bottom. The hand fans out cleanly at the bottom of the screen with clear visual differentiation between attacks (red) and skills (blue).

Status effects work correctly - Bash applies Vulnerable which is shown as a numbered badge below the enemy HP bar, and subsequent attacks properly deal 50% increased damage. The slime split mechanic (medium slimes splitting into two small slimes on death) is implemented faithfully, which shows real attention to source material detail.

"The slime split on death. In a browser game. That's commitment to the source material."

Multi-enemy targeting is handled via a "SELECT TARGET" banner with yellow-bordered enemy highlights - functional but not flashy. Single-enemy fights auto-target, which is the correct UX choice.

What Works
Energy system, card draw, damage calculation, Vulnerable modifier (50% bonus), enemy intents, slime splitting, auto-targeting vs manual targeting, draw/discard pile tracking, and the End Turn button all function as expected.

COMBAT UX ISSUES

  • Card damage preview does NOT update when enemies have Vulnerable - shows base damage (6) instead of effective damage (9). In StS, the number turns green when boosted.
  • Enemy intent shows a generic "Debuff" label without specifying WHAT debuff will be applied (Weak? Slimed? Frail?). Players need this info to make informed decisions.
  • No hover/tooltip on enemy status effect badges - the "2" below the HP bar means "2 turns of Vulnerable" but this isn't explained anywhere.
  • No damage numbers floating up when attacks connect - combat lacks satisfying feedback.
  • The "swipe" hint text at bottom-right is confusing for mouse users.

Rewards & Progression 7/10

Post-combat reward screens are well-structured with clear gold amounts and a card selection interface. The three-card choice spread feels good, with each card displaying its energy cost, type, and effect text. A "Skip Reward" button is available for players who don't want to dilute their deck.

The card selection has a notable text truncation bug: "Infernal Blade" renders as "Infernal Bla..." on the selection card. Card descriptions are also quite small and difficult to read, especially for cards with longer effect text. There's no hover-to-enlarge functionality that would help players make informed choices.

The starting relic correctly heals 6 HP after each combat (faithful to Burning Blood from StS), which is shown by HP increasing between the combat end and reward screen.

Art & Audio 6/10

Card art is AI-generated with a consistent dark fantasy aesthetic. Each card has a unique illustration that matches its theme - Strike shows a sword slash, Defend shows a shield ward, Shrug It Off depicts an armoured warrior. The art quality is surprisingly coherent across the deck, though the AI origins are visible in occasional anatomical oddities.

Enemy art uses WebP format with proper fallbacks. The Acid Slime monsters have appropriately goopy, menacing illustrations. The art pipeline includes a compression script for optimised delivery.

Audio is implemented via a dedicated audio system with mute toggle (visible in the header). In our testing session, no audio actually played - the system appears to be wired up but silent. The mute button's presence suggests sound was intended but may not be fully implemented in this build.

Technical note: The art pipeline uses Sharp for image compression and supports both WebP and PNG formats. Card art files are lazily imported via Vite's asset system, meaning each card art is a separate network request (~50-90KB each) on first load. The initial load fetches 100+ card art files.

Known Issues Critical

This is an alpha build, and it shows. The development team has documented several P0 (critical) bugs that significantly impact gameplay:

P0 - Game-Breaking Bugs

Bug Severity Impact
Potion UI calls non-existent action P0 Potions are completely non-functional. Potion slots visible but clicking does nothing.
Save/load data format mismatch P0 Saving and loading a run will corrupt game state. Progress cannot be preserved.
Card effects reference missing context P0 Some card effects crash when played because they reference a hand array that isn't passed to the effect context.

P1/P2 - Notable Issues

Bug Severity Impact
Asset format PNG/WebP mismatch P1 Some art assets fail to load, showing broken images.
Barricade only applies to one enemy P2 Block retention mechanic incomplete.
Fragile test selectors P2 Tests use regex HP matching instead of data-testid.

These P0 bugs mean the game is essentially unfinishable in certain scenarios. Cards that reference the hand context will crash the run, potions are decoration-only, and you can't save progress. The development team is aware and has these prioritised for the next sprint.

UX Deep Dive 5/10

INFORMATION HIERARCHY

The persistent header bar is well-designed, showing Act/Floor, HP, Gold, and deck size at a glance. Relic and Potion slots are visible below. The combat HUD clearly shows Draw pile, Discard pile, Energy, and the End Turn button in consistent positions.

WHAT'S MISSING

  • No card hover/enlarge preview - critical for reading card effects
  • No deck viewer accessible during combat or from the map
  • No tooltip system for relics, potions, or status effects
  • No damage/block animation feedback (numbers just change)
  • No visual distinction between common/uncommon/rare cards in selection
  • Enemy "Debuff" intent doesn't specify which debuff
  • Card names can truncate without any resize or tooltip fallback
  • No confirmation when skipping card rewards
  • Map doesn't indicate which nodes you've already visited
  • No run statistics or history screen

WHAT WORKS WELL

  • Clear card type distinction (red attacks, blue skills)
  • Target selection with banner prompt is intuitive
  • Energy cost clearly visible on each card
  • End Turn button is large and unambiguous
  • Reward screen structure (collect gold, pick card, continue)
  • Map legend explaining all node types

The Bottom Line

The Good

  • Faithful StS combat implementation
  • 60+ cards with unique AI art
  • Slime split mechanic - attention to detail
  • Clean card type visual language
  • Proper energy/draw/discard cycle
  • Target selection for multi-enemy
  • 15-floor acts with node variety
  • Relic healing works correctly
  • Browser-based - no install needed
  • Solid React architecture

The Bad

  • 3 P0 game-breaking bugs
  • Potions completely non-functional
  • Save/load is broken
  • Some cards crash the game
  • No tooltips or card enlargement
  • Damage preview ignores modifiers
  • Extremely dark - visibility issues
  • No combat feedback animations
  • Card text truncation
  • Audio silent in current build

Spire Ascent has the bones of a genuinely compelling browser deckbuilder. The combat system works, the card variety is impressive for an alpha, and the attention to source material detail (slime splitting!) shows a team that cares about getting it right. But three P0 bugs make the game unfinishable in many scenarios, the UX lacks the polish needed for accessible play (no tooltips, no card enlargement, no damage previews), and the visual feedback is too minimal to make combat feel satisfying. Fix the critical bugs, add basic tooltip infrastructure, and brighten the theme by about 20% - and this could easily climb into the 70s. It's a promising foundation that needs another couple of sprints of stabilisation before it's ready for real playtesting.
